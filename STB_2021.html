📌 Q1 Borel-Cantelli Lemmas & Almost Sure Convergence (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: For $n \geq 1$, $X_n$ are independent random variables with the probability distribution:
$$ X_n = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{with probability } (1 - \frac{1}{n}) \\ \frac{1}{n} & \text{with probability } \frac{1}{n}(1 - \frac{1}{n}) \\ n & \text{with probability } \frac{1}{n^2} \end{cases} $$
(a) Show that $\{X_n = n\}$ occurs only finitely many times with probability 1. Also show that $\{X_n = \frac{1}{n}\}$ occurs infinitely often with probability 1.
(b) Determine if the sequence $\{X_n\}$ converges almost surely or not.

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

This problem directly tests your understanding of the Borel-Cantelli Lemmas. To determine if an event happens infinitely often (i.o.) or finitely many times, we examine the infinite series of their probabilities. If the sum converges, the event occurs finitely many times almost surely (First Lemma). If the sum diverges and the events are independent, it occurs infinitely often almost surely (Second Lemma). For part (b), we leverage these limiting behaviors to establish pointwise limits for the random sequence.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Step 1: First Borel-Cantelli Lemma for $\{X_n = n\}$

Let $A_n$ be the event that $\{X_n = n\}$. The probability of this event occurring is strictly given by:

$$ P(A_n) = \frac{1}{n^2} $$

We analyze the infinite sum of these probabilities:

$$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6} < \infty $$

Because the sum converges to a finite value, the First Borel-Cantelli Lemma guarantees that the probability of $A_n$ occurring infinitely often is zero. Thus, $P(\limsup_{n \to \infty} A_n) = 0$, meaning the event $\{X_n = n\}$ occurs only finitely many times with probability 1.

Step 2: Second Borel-Cantelli Lemma for $\{X_n = 1/n\}$

Let $B_n$ be the event that $\{X_n = \frac{1}{n}\}$. The probability is:

$$ P(B_n) = \frac{1}{n} \left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right) = \frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n^2} $$

We analyze the infinite sum of these probabilities:

$$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(B_n) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( \frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n^2} \right) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} - \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} $$

Since the harmonic series $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ strictly diverges to $\infty$ and $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$ is a finite constant, the total sum diverges: $\sum P(B_n) = \infty$. Furthermore, the problem explicitly states that the random variables $X_n$ are independent. By the Second Borel-Cantelli Lemma, $P(\limsup_{n \to \infty} B_n) = 1$. Therefore, $\{X_n = \frac{1}{n}\}$ occurs infinitely often with probability 1.

Step 3: Determining Almost Sure Convergence (Part b)

We must determine if $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} X$. Let's examine the sample paths $X_n(\omega)$ for an arbitrary outcome $\omega \in \Omega$, excluding the probability 0 set where $A_n$ occurs infinitely often. For almost all $\omega$, there exists an integer $N(\omega)$ such that for all $n > N(\omega)$, the event $A_n$ does not occur.

Therefore, for all $n > N(\omega)$, $X_n(\omega)$ strictly takes values from the set $\{0, \frac{1}{n}\}$.

Since both candidate values in the tail sequence strictly converge to 0 (i.e., $\lim_{n \to \infty} 0 = 0$ and $\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} = 0$), the sequence itself must squeeze to zero:

$$ \lim_{n \to \infty} X_n(\omega) = 0 \quad \text{for almost all } \omega $$
Final Answer / Q.E.D:
(a) By the 1st Borel-Cantelli Lemma, $\sum \frac{1}{n^2} < \infty \implies \{X_n=n\}$ occurs finitely many times a.s. By the 2nd Lemma, because of independence and $\sum (\frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n^2}) = \infty$, $\{X_n=1/n\}$ occurs infinitely often a.s.
(b) Because the explosive term $n$ vanishes for large $n$, and the remaining states $0$ and $1/n$ both trivially tend to $0$, the sequence strictly converges almost surely to $0$ ($X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$).

📌 Q2 Normal MLE with Integer Parameter Space (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: Let $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n$ be a random sample from $N(\theta, 1)$ where $\theta$ is an unknown integer.
(a) Find the maximum likelihood estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ of $\theta$.
(b) Prove that it is not possible to find a sequence of real constants $a_n$ such that $a_n(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)$ converges to a non-degenerate limiting distribution.

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

This problem introduces a restriction on the parameter space: $\theta \in \mathbb{Z}$. Standard calculus derivatives cannot strictly optimize a discrete parameter space. Instead, we algebraically maximize the likelihood by minimizing the spatial distance between the sample mean and the integer candidates. For part (b), because the true parameter is an integer, as the sample size grows, the standard continuous MLE ($\bar{X}$) becomes so "tight" around $\theta$ that the rounded discrete MLE will exactly equal the true parameter with a probability converging to 1, causing any scaled distribution to collapse into a degenerate point mass.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Step 1: Finding the MLE (Part a)

The joint likelihood function for a Normal distribution with unit variance is:

$$ L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}(X_i - \theta)^2\right) \propto \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (X_i - \theta)^2\right) $$

We can rewrite the sum of squares by introducing the sample mean $\bar{X}$:

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} (X_i - \theta)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} ((X_i - \bar{X}) + (\bar{X} - \theta))^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (X_i - \bar{X})^2 + n(\bar{X} - \theta)^2 $$

To maximize $L(\theta)$, we must strictly minimize the exponent component $n(\bar{X} - \theta)^2$. Thus, we need to find the integer $\theta$ that is closest to the continuous sample mean $\bar{X}$.

The MLE is the nearest integer function applied to the sample mean:

$$ \hat{\theta}_n = \lfloor \bar{X} \rceil \quad (\text{or mathematically: } \text{argmin}_{\theta \in \mathbb{Z}} |\bar{X} - \theta|) $$

Step 2: Analyzing the Distribution of the MLE (Part b)

Because $X_i \sim N(\theta, 1)$ i.i.d., the sample mean has the exact distribution $\bar{X} \sim N(\theta, \frac{1}{n})$. Therefore, the standardized distance is exactly normal:

$$ \sqrt{n}(\bar{X} - \theta) \sim N(0, 1) $$

The discrete MLE $\hat{\theta}_n$ will exactly equal the true integer parameter $\theta$ if and only if $\bar{X}$ is closer to $\theta$ than any other integer. This happens when:

$$ \bar{X} \in (\theta - 0.5, \theta + 0.5) $$

Step 3: Calculating the Asymptotic Probability

Let's find the probability that the estimator makes an error of zero:

$$ P(\hat{\theta}_n = \theta) = P(-0.5 < \bar{X} - \theta < 0.5) $$

Multiply the inequality by $\sqrt{n}$ to transform the inner variable to the standard normal $Z$:

$$ P(\hat{\theta}_n = \theta) = P\left(-0.5\sqrt{n} < Z < 0.5\sqrt{n}\right) $$

As $n \to \infty$, the boundaries $-0.5\sqrt{n} \to -\infty$ and $0.5\sqrt{n} \to \infty$. Thus:

$$ \lim_{n \to \infty} P(\hat{\theta}_n = \theta) = P(-\infty < Z < \infty) = 1 $$

Step 4: Implication for any Sequence $a_n$

We are tasked with examining the random variable $Y_n = a_n(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)$.

Because $P(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta = 0) \to 1$, the random variable $Y_n$ evaluates exactly to $a_n(0) = 0$ with probability approaching 1, absolutely regardless of how fast or slow the sequence of constants $a_n$ grows.

Therefore, $Y_n \xrightarrow{P} 0$, which strictly implies that $Y_n$ converges in distribution to a degenerate point mass at 0. It is structurally impossible to obtain a non-degenerate distribution.

Final Answer / Q.E.D:
(a) The MLE is the nearest integer to the sample mean: $\hat{\theta}_n = \lfloor \bar{X} \rceil$.
(b) Because the probability of the estimator exactly hitting the true parameter converges to 1 ($P(\hat{\theta}_n = \theta) \to 1$), the scaled difference $a_n(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)$ strictly collapses to a degenerate mass at 0 for any sequence $a_n$.

📌 Q3 Linear Estimation and Confidence Regions (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: Suppose the observable random vector $(Y_1, Y_2, \dots, Y_5)'$ is normally distributed with mean $( (\alpha+\beta), (\alpha+\beta), (\alpha-\gamma), (\alpha-\gamma), (\delta-\alpha) )'$ and covariance matrix $\sigma^2 I$. $\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta, \sigma^2$ are fixed unknown parameters.
(a) Find the class of linear unbiased estimators of $(\delta - \alpha)$.
(b) For observed $(y_1, \dots, y_5)' = (10, 12, 6, 10, 22)'$, find a $100(1-\theta)\%$ confidence region for $(\alpha+\beta, \alpha-\gamma)'$ in terms of a quantile of a known distribution, where $0 < \theta < 1$.

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

This is a classic Linear Models (Gauss-Markov) problem.

  • Part (a): To find a linear unbiased estimator, we propose a linear combination $\mathbf{c}^T Y = \sum c_i Y_i$, take its expectation, equate it strictly to the target parameter $\delta - \alpha$, and solve the resulting system of linear constraints on the coefficients $c_i$.
  • Part (b): We recognize the means as grouped parameters: $\phi_1 = \alpha+\beta$ and $\phi_2 = \alpha-\gamma$. We estimate these parameters via localized sample means, calculate the Error Sum of Squares (SSE) to estimate $\sigma^2$, and construct an $F$-statistic to generate an elliptical joint confidence region.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Step 1: Finding Linear Unbiased Estimators (Part a)

Let an arbitrary linear estimator be $T = c_1Y_1 + c_2Y_2 + c_3Y_3 + c_4Y_4 + c_5Y_5$. Its expectation must strictly equal $\delta - \alpha$ for all possible values of the parameters:

$$ \mathbb{E}(T) = c_1(\alpha+\beta) + c_2(\alpha+\beta) + c_3(\alpha-\gamma) + c_4(\alpha-\gamma) + c_5(\delta-\alpha) $$

Group the coefficients by the Greek parameters:

$$ \mathbb{E}(T) = \alpha(c_1+c_2+c_3+c_4-c_5) + \beta(c_1+c_2) + \gamma(-c_3-c_4) + \delta(c_5) \equiv -\alpha + \delta $$

By equating coefficients, we obtain a linear system:

  • For $\delta$: $c_5 = 1$
  • For $\beta$: $c_1 + c_2 = 0 \implies c_2 = -c_1$
  • For $\gamma$: $-c_3 - c_4 = 0 \implies c_4 = -c_3$
  • For $\alpha$: $c_1 + c_2 + c_3 + c_4 - c_5 = -1$. Substituting the previous constraints yields $0 + 0 - 1 = -1$, which is perfectly consistent.

Thus, the class of linear unbiased estimators is given by any linear combination of the form:

$$ T = c_1(Y_1 - Y_2) + c_3(Y_3 - Y_4) + Y_5 \quad \text{for any } c_1, c_3 \in \mathbb{R} $$

Step 2: Defining Parametric Groups and Least Squares (Part b)

Let $\phi_1 = \alpha+\beta$ and $\phi_2 = \alpha-\gamma$. Since $Y_1, Y_2$ share mean $\phi_1$, and $Y_3, Y_4$ share mean $\phi_2$, the optimal Least Squares Estimators (also MLEs for normal data) are their respective sample averages:

$$ \hat{\phi}_1 = \frac{Y_1 + Y_2}{2} = \frac{10 + 12}{2} = 11 $$
$$ \hat{\phi}_2 = \frac{Y_3 + Y_4}{2} = \frac{6 + 10}{2} = 8 $$

The variance for each of these estimates is exactly $\frac{\sigma^2}{2}$, and they are strictly independent.

Step 3: Calculating Error Sum of Squares (SSE)

The SSE is the sum of squared residuals from the estimated means. Notice $Y_5$ fits its mean $\delta-\alpha$ perfectly, so it contributes 0 to the error variance. The error degrees of freedom is $n - \text{rank}(X) = 5 - 3 = 2$.

$$ SSE = \sum (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2 = (Y_1 - \hat{\phi}_1)^2 + (Y_2 - \hat{\phi}_1)^2 + (Y_3 - \hat{\phi}_2)^2 + (Y_4 - \hat{\phi}_2)^2 $$

Plugging in the observed data:

$$ SSE = (10 - 11)^2 + (12 - 11)^2 + (6 - 8)^2 + (10 - 8)^2 = (-1)^2 + (1)^2 + (-2)^2 + (2)^2 = 1 + 1 + 4 + 4 = 10 $$

We compute the Mean Squared Error ($MSE$): $MSE = SSE / \text{df}_{error} = 10 / 2 = 5$.

Step 4: Constructing the F-Statistic

Because $\hat{\phi}_1 \sim N(\phi_1, \sigma^2/2)$ and $\hat{\phi}_2 \sim N(\phi_2, \sigma^2/2)$ independently, the sum of their squared standardizations follows a $\chi^2$ distribution with 2 degrees of freedom:

$$ \frac{(\hat{\phi}_1 - \phi_1)^2}{\sigma^2/2} + \frac{(\hat{\phi}_2 - \phi_2)^2}{\sigma^2/2} \sim \chi^2_2 \implies \frac{2}{\sigma^2} [(\hat{\phi}_1 - \phi_1)^2 + (\hat{\phi}_2 - \phi_2)^2] \sim \chi^2_2 $$

We divide this by its degrees of freedom (2), and then divide by the independent error term $(SSE/\sigma^2)/2$ to form the $F$-statistic:

$$ F = \frac{\frac{1}{\sigma^2} [(\hat{\phi}_1 - \phi_1)^2 + (\hat{\phi}_2 - \phi_2)^2]}{MSE / \sigma^2} = \frac{(\hat{\phi}_1 - \phi_1)^2 + (\hat{\phi}_2 - \phi_2)^2}{MSE} \sim F_{2, 2} $$

Substituting our empirical values $\hat{\phi}_1 = 11$, $\hat{\phi}_2 = 8$, and $MSE = 5$, the bounding probability equation is $P(F \leq F_{2, 2, \theta}) = 1 - \theta$, where $F_{2, 2, \theta}$ is the upper $\theta$ quantile of the $F$-distribution.

Final Answer / Q.E.D:
(a) The class of linear unbiased estimators for $\delta - \alpha$ is exactly $\{c_1(Y_1 - Y_2) + c_3(Y_3 - Y_4) + Y_5\}$ for any real $c_1, c_3$.
(b) The $100(1-\theta)\%$ confidence region for the vector $(\alpha+\beta, \alpha-\gamma)'$ is the set of all points satisfying the elliptical inequality:
$$ (\alpha+\beta - 11)^2 + (\alpha-\gamma - 8)^2 \leq 5 F_{2, 2, \theta} $$
