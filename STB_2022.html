📌 Q1 Most Powerful and UMP Tests for Normal Distributions (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: Let $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_m$ and $Y_1, Y_2, \dots, Y_n$ be independent random samples from $N(\mu, \sigma_0^2)$ and $N(2\mu, \sigma_0^2)$, respectively, where $\sigma_0 (>0)$ is known and $-\infty < \mu < \infty$ is unknown.
(a) Find the Most Powerful level $\alpha$ test for testing $H_0: \mu = 0$ vs. $H_1: \mu = 1$.
(b) Is the test in (a) also the Uniformly Most Powerful level $\alpha$ test for testing $H_0: \mu \leq 0$ versus $H_1: \mu > 0$?

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

Part (a) requires the application of the Neyman-Pearson Lemma, which states that the Most Powerful (MP) test for simple hypotheses is based on the likelihood ratio $\Lambda = L(\theta_1)/L(\theta_0)$. By expanding the joint likelihood function of both samples and simplifying the ratio, we can identify a sufficient test statistic $T$. For Part (b), we check if the joint density belongs to the one-parameter exponential family and exhibits a Monotone Likelihood Ratio (MLR). If it does, the Karlin-Rubin Theorem guarantees that our threshold test is Uniformly Most Powerful (UMP) for the composite one-sided alternative.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Step 1: Construct the Joint Likelihood Function

Since the samples are independent, the joint likelihood function $L(\mu)$ is the product of their individual probability density functions:

$$L(\mu) = \prod_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{\sigma_0\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma_0^2}\right) \prod_{j=1}^n \frac{1}{\sigma_0\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(y_j - 2\mu)^2}{2\sigma_0^2}\right)$$
$$L(\mu) = (2\pi\sigma_0^2)^{-\frac{m+n}{2}} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^m (x_i - \mu)^2 + \sum_{j=1}^n (y_j - 2\mu)^2 \right] \right)$$

Step 2: Apply the Neyman-Pearson Lemma

We evaluate the likelihood ratio for $H_0: \mu=0$ against $H_1: \mu=1$. The MP test rejects $H_0$ if $L(1)/L(0) > k$ for some constant $k$.

$$\frac{L(1)}{L(0)} = \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^m (x_i - 1)^2 + \sum_{j=1}^n (y_j - 2)^2 - \sum_{i=1}^m x_i^2 - \sum_{j=1}^n y_j^2 \right] \right)$$

Expanding the squares and canceling the $x_i^2$ and $y_j^2$ terms:

$$\sum_{i=1}^m (x_i^2 - 2x_i + 1) - \sum_{i=1}^m x_i^2 = -2\sum_{i=1}^m x_i + m$$
$$\sum_{j=1}^n (y_j^2 - 4y_j + 4) - \sum_{j=1}^n y_j^2 = -4\sum_{j=1}^n y_j + 4n$$

Substituting this back into the likelihood ratio:

$$\frac{L(1)}{L(0)} = \exp\left( \frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2} \left[ 2\sum_{i=1}^m x_i + 4\sum_{j=1}^n y_j - (m + 4n) \right] \right)$$
$$\frac{L(1)}{L(0)} = \exp\left( \frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} \left( \sum_{i=1}^m x_i + 2\sum_{j=1}^n y_j \right) - \frac{m+4n}{2\sigma_0^2} \right)$$

Because the exponential function is strictly increasing, rejecting $H_0$ for large values of the likelihood ratio is mathematically equivalent to rejecting $H_0$ for large values of the statistic $T = \sum_{i=1}^m X_i + 2\sum_{j=1}^n Y_j$. Therefore, our critical region is $T > c$.

Step 3: Determine the Null Distribution and Critical Value

Under $H_0$ ($\mu = 0$), we have $X_i \sim N(0, \sigma_0^2)$ and $Y_j \sim N(0, \sigma_0^2)$. Because $T$ is a linear combination of independent normal variables, it is also normally distributed.

$$E_{H_0}[T] = \sum E[X_i] + 2\sum E[Y_j] = 0$$
$$Var_{H_0}(T) = \sum Var(X_i) + 4\sum Var(Y_j) = m\sigma_0^2 + 4n\sigma_0^2 = (m + 4n)\sigma_0^2$$

Thus, under $H_0$, $T \sim N(0, (m+4n)\sigma_0^2)$. To achieve a level $\alpha$ test, we standardize $T$:

$$P_{H_0}(T > c) = \alpha \implies P\left( \frac{T}{\sigma_0\sqrt{m+4n}} > \frac{c}{\sigma_0\sqrt{m+4n}} \right) = \alpha$$

Where $Z \sim N(0,1)$. Thus, the threshold is $c = z_\alpha \sigma_0 \sqrt{m+4n}$, where $z_\alpha$ is the upper $\alpha$-quantile of the standard normal distribution.

Step 4: Proving UMP for Composite Hypotheses (Part b)

We factor the general likelihood function $L(\mu)$ for any $\mu$ to check for the Monotone Likelihood Ratio (MLR) property:

$$L(\mu) = C(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2} [ -2\mu\sum x_i + m\mu^2 - 4\mu\sum y_j + 4n\mu^2 ] \right)$$
$$L(\mu) = C(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \exp\left( \frac{\mu}{\sigma_0^2} \left( \sum x_i + 2\sum y_j \right) - \frac{\mu^2(m+4n)}{2\sigma_0^2} \right)$$

This demonstrates that the joint distribution forms a one-parameter exponential family with $T = \sum x_i + 2\sum y_j$ as the sufficient statistic. Because the coefficient of $T$ is $\frac{\mu}{\sigma_0^2}$, which is a strictly increasing function of $\mu$, the family possesses an MLR in $T$.

By the Karlin-Rubin Theorem, a test of the form "Reject $H_0$ if $T > c$" is the Uniformly Most Powerful test for testing $H_0: \mu \leq \mu_0$ versus $H_1: \mu > \mu_0$. Setting $\mu_0 = 0$ perfectly matches our scenario.

Final Answer / Q.E.D:
(a) The Most Powerful level $\alpha$ test rejects $H_0$ if $T = \sum_{i=1}^m X_i + 2\sum_{j=1}^n Y_j > z_\alpha \sigma_0 \sqrt{m+4n}$.
(b) Yes. By the Karlin-Rubin Theorem, because the distribution has a Monotone Likelihood Ratio in $T$, this exact same test is the UMP test for the composite one-sided alternative.

📌 Q2 Unbiasedness and UMVUE for Poisson Distribution (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: Suppose $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, $\lambda > 0$. Let $\theta = \lambda^3 \exp(-2\lambda)$.
(a) Show that $S = (-1)^{X-1}X(X-1)(X-2)$ is an unbiased estimate of $\theta$.
(b) Consider a random sample $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n$ from $\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$. Derive the Uniformly Minimum Variance Unbiased Estimator (UMVUE) of $\theta$.

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

Part (a) is an algebraic verification exercise utilizing the definition of expectation and the Taylor series expansion of $e^x$. For Part (b), we rely on the Lehmann-Scheffé Theorem. We first identify a complete sufficient statistic for $\lambda$ in the Poisson distribution, which is the sample sum $T = \sum X_i$. Then, we construct a function $g(T)$ such that its expectation exactly equals the parameter $\theta = \lambda^3 e^{-2\lambda}$. Because $T$ is complete and sufficient, this unbiased $g(T)$ is guaranteed to be the unique UMVUE.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Step 1: Verify Unbiasedness of $S$ (Part a)

The expectation of $S$ is computed using the PMF of the Poisson distribution, $P(X = x) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x}{x!}$:

$$E[S] = \sum_{x=0}^{\infty} (-1)^{x-1} x(x-1)(x-2) \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x}{x!}$$

Notice that for $x = 0, 1, 2$, the term $x(x-1)(x-2)$ evaluates to $0$. Therefore, we can start the summation index from $x = 3$. This allows us to cancel terms in the factorial $x! = x(x-1)(x-2)(x-3)!$:

$$E[S] = \sum_{x=3}^{\infty} (-1)^{x-1} \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x}{(x-3)!}$$

To evaluate this series, let's re-index by setting $y = x - 3$. As $x$ goes from 3 to $\infty$, $y$ goes from 0 to $\infty$. We replace $x$ with $y + 3$:

$$E[S] = \sum_{y=0}^{\infty} (-1)^{y+2} \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^{y+3}}{y!}$$

We factor out constants with respect to $y$, noting that $(-1)^{y+2} = (-1)^y (-1)^2 = (-1)^y$:

$$E[S] = e^{-\lambda} \lambda^3 \sum_{y=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-\lambda)^y}{y!}$$

We recognize the sum as the Maclaurin series expansion for $e^{-\lambda}$ (since $\sum \frac{z^y}{y!} = e^z$ for $z = -\lambda$):

$$E[S] = e^{-\lambda} \lambda^3 (e^{-\lambda}) = \lambda^3 e^{-2\lambda} = \theta$$

Thus, $S$ is an unbiased estimator of $\theta$.

Step 2: Identify Complete Sufficient Statistic (Part b)

For a random sample from a Poisson distribution, the sum of the observations $T = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ is a complete and sufficient statistic for $\lambda$. Furthermore, by the additive property of independent Poisson variables, $T \sim \text{Poisson}(n\lambda)$. Its PMF is $P(T = t) = \frac{e^{-n\lambda}(n\lambda)^t}{t!}$.

Step 3: Construct the UMVUE via Expectation Matching

By the Lehmann-Scheffé Theorem, we must find a function $g(T)$ such that $E[g(T)] = \theta$:

$$\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} g(t) \frac{e^{-n\lambda}(n\lambda)^t}{t!} = \lambda^3 e^{-2\lambda}$$

Multiply both sides by $e^{n\lambda}$:

$$\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} g(t) \frac{n^t}{t!} \lambda^t = \lambda^3 e^{(n-2)\lambda}$$

We expand the right side using the Taylor series for the exponential function, $e^{(n-2)\lambda} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{((n-2)\lambda)^k}{k!}$:

$$\text{RHS} = \lambda^3 \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(n-2)^k}{k!} \lambda^k = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(n-2)^k}{k!} \lambda^{k+3}$$

To match the powers of $\lambda$ on both sides, let's re-index the right side by setting $t = k + 3$, which means $k = t - 3$. As $k$ starts from $0$, $t$ starts from $3$:

$$\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} g(t) \frac{n^t}{t!} \lambda^t = \sum_{t=3}^{\infty} \frac{(n-2)^{t-3}}{(t-3)!} \lambda^t$$

Step 4: Solve for $g(t)$

Because this equality must hold for all $\lambda > 0$, the coefficients of $\lambda^t$ on both sides must be strictly equal.

  • For $t = 0, 1, 2$: The right side has no terms for these powers of $\lambda$, implying the coefficient is 0. Thus, $g(t) = 0$.
  • For $t \geq 3$: Equating the coefficients yields:
$$g(t) \frac{n^t}{t!} = \frac{(n-2)^{t-3}}{(t-3)!}$$

Isolating $g(t)$:

$$g(t) = \frac{t!}{n^t(t-3)!} (n-2)^{t-3}$$

Expanding the factorial $t! / (t-3)! = t(t-1)(t-2)$ and rearranging the terms algebraically:

$$g(t) = \frac{t(t-1)(t-2)}{n^3 \cdot n^{t-3}} (n-2)^{t-3} = \frac{t(t-1)(t-2)}{n^3} \left( \frac{n-2}{n} \right)^{t-3}$$
Final Answer / Q.E.D:
The Uniformly Minimum Variance Unbiased Estimator (UMVUE) of $\theta$ is:
$$g(T) = \begin{cases} \frac{T(T-1)(T-2)}{n^3} \left( 1 - \frac{2}{n} \right)^{T-3} & \text{if } T \geq 3 \\ 0 & \text{if } T < 3 \end{cases}$$ where $T = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$.

📌 Q3 Asymptotic Probability using Central Limit Theorem (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: Let $X_1, X_2, \dots$ be a sequence of independent and identically distributed $\text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ random variables. If $Z_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2$, find: $$\lim_{n \to \infty} P\left(Z_n \leq \frac{n}{3} + \left(\frac{2n}{45}\right)^{1/2}\right)$$

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

This problem is a direct application of the Lindeberg-Lévy Central Limit Theorem (CLT). The statistic $Z_n$ is a sum of i.i.d. random variables $Y_i = X_i^2$. We first compute the mean and variance of the transformed variable $Y_i$. Once we establish the true mean and standard deviation of the sum $Z_n$, we standardize the probability expression. As $n \to \infty$, the standardized variable converges in distribution to a standard normal variable, allowing us to evaluate the limit using the standard normal CDF $\Phi(z)$.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Step 1: Calculate Moments of $Y_i = X_i^2$

Since $X_i \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$, its PDF is $f(x) = 1$ for $x \in (0, 1)$. We find the first and second moments of $Y_i$:

$$E[Y_i] = E[X_i^2] = \int_{0}^{1} x^2 \cdot 1 \, dx = \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} \right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{3}$$
$$E[Y_i^2] = E[(X_i^2)^2] = E[X_i^4] = \int_{0}^{1} x^4 \cdot 1 \, dx = \left[ \frac{x^5}{5} \right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{5}$$

Now, compute the variance of $Y_i$:

$$Var(Y_i) = E[Y_i^2] - (E[Y_i])^2 = \frac{1}{5} - \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{9} = \frac{9 - 5}{45} = \frac{4}{45}$$

Step 2: Apply the Central Limit Theorem

Since $Z_n = \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$ is the sum of $n$ i.i.d. random variables with finite mean $\mu = 1/3$ and variance $\sigma^2 = 4/45$, the Central Limit Theorem asserts that:

$$\frac{Z_n - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1) \text{ as } n \to \infty$$

Substituting our calculated values for $\mu$ and $\sigma$:

$$W_n = \frac{Z_n - n(1/3)}{\sqrt{n(4/45)}} = \frac{Z_n - n/3}{\sqrt{4n/45}} \xrightarrow{d} Z \sim N(0, 1)$$

Step 3: Standardize the Probability Expression

We are asked to evaluate $\lim_{n \to \infty} P\left(Z_n \leq \frac{n}{3} + \sqrt{\frac{2n}{45}}\right)$. We manipulate the inequality to match the form of $W_n$:

$$P\left(Z_n - \frac{n}{3} \leq \sqrt{\frac{2n}{45}}\right)$$

Divide both sides by the standard deviation of $Z_n$, which is $\sqrt{4n/45}$:

$$P\left( \frac{Z_n - n/3}{\sqrt{4n/45}} \leq \frac{\sqrt{2n/45}}{\sqrt{4n/45}} \right) = P\left( W_n \leq \sqrt{\frac{2n/45}{4n/45}} \right)$$

Simplifying the ratio on the right-hand side:

$$\sqrt{\frac{2n/45}{4n/45}} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{4}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$

Therefore, the expression becomes $\lim_{n \to \infty} P\left( W_n \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \right)$. By the definition of convergence in distribution to standard normal $Z$:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} P\left( W_n \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \right) = P\left( Z \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \right) = \Phi\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)$$
Final Answer / Q.E.D: The limit evaluates to $\Phi\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)$, where $\Phi$ is the cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution.

📌 Q4 Asymptotic Confidence Interval for Trinomial Distribution (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: Suppose $\mathbf{X}_1, \dots, \mathbf{X}_m$ is a random sample from a Trinomial distribution with $n=1$, and $\pi = [\pi_1, \pi_2, \pi_3]^T$, where $0 < \pi_1, \pi_2, \pi_3 < 1$, and $\pi_1 + \pi_2 + \pi_3 = 1$. Find an approximate 95% confidence interval for $\pi_1 + \frac{1}{2}\pi_2$, for large $m$.

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

This problem calls for the application of asymptotic theory, specifically the Multivariate Central Limit Theorem (CLT) combined with the Delta Method (or simply the linear combination of asymptotically normal variables). Since $n=1$, each observation $\mathbf{X}_i$ is a single categorical trial. We will first establish the asymptotic joint distribution of the sample proportions. Then, we will define a transformation function $g(\pi) = \pi_1 + \frac{1}{2}\pi_2$, find its gradient, and compute the asymptotic variance of our plug-in estimator. This level of rigorous derivation is essential when developing a comprehensive collection of model solutions for advanced statistical inference.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Step 1: Define the Moments of the Trinomial Vector

Let $\mathbf{X}_i = (X_{i1}, X_{i2}, X_{i3})^T$. For a single trial ($n=1$), $X_{ij}$ acts as an indicator variable for category $j$. The expectation and covariance matrix of $\mathbf{X}_i$ are given by standard Multinomial properties:

$$E[\mathbf{X}_i] = \pi = \begin{pmatrix} \pi_1 \\ \pi_2 \\ \pi_3 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \Sigma = Var(\mathbf{X}_i) = \begin{pmatrix} \pi_1(1-\pi_1) & -\pi_1\pi_2 & -\pi_1\pi_3 \\ -\pi_1\pi_2 & \pi_2(1-\pi_2) & -\pi_2\pi_3 \\ -\pi_1\pi_3 & -\pi_2\pi_3 & \pi_3(1-\pi_3) \end{pmatrix}$$

Step 2: Apply the Multivariate Central Limit Theorem

Let the sample mean vector be $\hat{\pi} = \bar{\mathbf{X}} = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m \mathbf{X}_i$. By the Multivariate CLT, as $m \to \infty$:

$$\sqrt{m}(\hat{\pi} - \pi) \xrightarrow{d} N_3(\mathbf{0}, \Sigma)$$

Step 3: Linear Transformation for the Target Parameter

We want to estimate $\theta = \pi_1 + \frac{1}{2}\pi_2$. This is a linear combination of the components of $\pi$, which can be written as $\theta = \mathbf{a}^T \pi$, where $\mathbf{a} = (1, 1/2, 0)^T$. Our estimator is $\hat{\theta} = \mathbf{a}^T \hat{\pi} = \hat{\pi}_1 + \frac{1}{2}\hat{\pi}_2$.

By the continuous mapping theorem (or Delta Method for linear functions), the asymptotic distribution of $\hat{\theta}$ is:

$$\sqrt{m}(\hat{\theta} - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, V)$$

where the asymptotic variance $V = \mathbf{a}^T \Sigma \mathbf{a}$.

Step 4: Compute the Asymptotic Variance $V$

We perform the matrix multiplication $V = \mathbf{a}^T \Sigma \mathbf{a}$:

$$V = (1)^2 \Sigma_{11} + \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \Sigma_{22} + 2(1)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \Sigma_{12}$$
$$V = \pi_1(1-\pi_1) + \frac{1}{4}\pi_2(1-\pi_2) - \pi_1\pi_2$$

Expanding and regrouping the terms:

$$V = \pi_1 - \pi_1^2 + \frac{1}{4}\pi_2 - \frac{1}{4}\pi_2^2 - \pi_1\pi_2 = \left(\pi_1 + \frac{1}{4}\pi_2\right) - \left(\pi_1^2 + \pi_1\pi_2 + \frac{1}{4}\pi_2^2\right)$$

Notice that the second grouped term is a perfect square:

$$V = \left(\pi_1 + \frac{1}{4}\pi_2\right) - \left(\pi_1 + \frac{1}{2}\pi_2\right)^2$$

Step 5: Construct the Confidence Interval

By Slutsky's Theorem, we can substitute the consistent estimator $\hat{\pi}$ into $V$ to get a consistent estimator of the variance, $\hat{V}$.

$$\hat{V} = \hat{\pi}_1 + \frac{1}{4}\hat{\pi}_2 - \left(\hat{\pi}_1 + \frac{1}{2}\hat{\pi}_2\right)^2$$

The standardized statistic $\frac{\hat{\theta} - \theta}{\sqrt{\hat{V}/m}}$ converges in distribution to $N(0,1)$. For a 95% confidence interval, the critical value is $z_{0.025} \approx 1.96$.

Final Answer / Q.E.D: The approximate 95% confidence interval for $\pi_1 + \frac{1}{2}\pi_2$ is given by: $$\left( \hat{\pi}_1 + \frac{1}{2}\hat{\pi}_2 \right) \pm 1.96 \sqrt{ \frac{\left(\hat{\pi}_1 + \frac{1}{4}\hat{\pi}_2\right) - \left(\hat{\pi}_1 + \frac{1}{2}\hat{\pi}_2\right)^2}{m} }$$ where $\hat{\pi}_1$ and $\hat{\pi}_2$ are the sample proportions of categories 1 and 2, respectively.

📌 Q5 Markov Chain Marginals and Correlation (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: Let $Y_1, Y_2, \dots$ be categorical random variables (categories $C_1, \dots, C_k$). For $t \ge 2$, conditional probabilities are: $P(Y_t = C_i | Y_{t-1} = C_{j_1}, Y_{t-2} = C_{j_2}, \dots) = P(Y_t = C_i | Y_{t-1} = C_{j_1}) = (1-\alpha)p_i + \alpha I(i = j_1)$, and $P(Y_1 = C_i) = p_i$, where $0 \le \alpha \le 1$.
(a) Find the marginal distribution of $Y_t$ for $t \ge 2$.
(b) Find the conditional distribution of $Y_{t+h}$ given $Y_t$, for $h \ge 1$.
(c) If $C_i = i$, find the correlation coefficient between $Y_t$ and $Y_{t+h}$ for $h \ge 1$.

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

The problem defines a discrete-time Markov Chain with a specific transition mechanism: with probability $\alpha$, the state remains exactly the same as the previous step, and with probability $(1-\alpha)$, the state is completely redrawn from a baseline distribution $\mathbf{p}$. We will use matrix algebra and the Law of Total Probability to prove that $\mathbf{p}$ is the stationary distribution. For the $h$-step transitions, we will utilize the properties of the transition matrix, specifically looking at the spectral decomposition or simply mathematical induction. Finally, we compute moments to find the covariance and correlation.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Part (a): Marginal Distribution of $Y_t$

We proceed by mathematical induction. Base case: $P(Y_1 = C_i) = p_i$. Assume for $t-1$ that $P(Y_{t-1} = C_j) = p_j$. Using the Law of Total Probability for $Y_t$:

$$P(Y_t = C_i) = \sum_{j=1}^k P(Y_t = C_i | Y_{t-1} = C_j) P(Y_{t-1} = C_j)$$
$$P(Y_t = C_i) = \sum_{j=1}^k \left[ (1-\alpha)p_i + \alpha \delta_{ij} \right] p_j$$

where $\delta_{ij}$ is the Kronecker delta (equal to $I(i=j)$). Distributing the sum:

$$P(Y_t = C_i) = (1-\alpha)p_i \sum_{j=1}^k p_j + \alpha \sum_{j=1}^k \delta_{ij} p_j$$

Since $\sum_{j=1}^k p_j = 1$ and the delta function collapses the sum to just the $j=i$ term:

$$P(Y_t = C_i) = (1-\alpha)p_i(1) + \alpha p_i = p_i - \alpha p_i + \alpha p_i = p_i$$

Thus, by induction, the marginal distribution is $P(Y_t = C_i) = p_i$ for all $t \ge 1$.

Part (b): $h$-step Conditional Distribution

Let $P$ be the transition matrix with elements $P_{ji} = (1-\alpha)p_i + \alpha \delta_{ji}$. In matrix notation, $P = (1-\alpha)\mathbf{1}\mathbf{p}^T + \alpha I$, where $\mathbf{1}$ is a column vector of ones, $\mathbf{p}$ is the column vector of probabilities, and $I$ is the identity matrix.

Let $Q = \mathbf{1}\mathbf{p}^T$. Note that $Q^2 = (\mathbf{1}\mathbf{p}^T)(\mathbf{1}\mathbf{p}^T) = \mathbf{1}(\mathbf{p}^T\mathbf{1})\mathbf{p}^T$. Since $\mathbf{p}$ is a probability vector, its sum is 1, so $\mathbf{p}^T\mathbf{1} = 1$. Thus, $Q^2 = Q$ (it is an idempotent matrix representing the steady-state). Because $I$ commutes with any matrix, we can easily find $P^h$:

We claim $P^h = (1-\alpha^h)Q + \alpha^h I$. We prove by induction. True for $h=1$. Assume for $h$. For $h+1$:

$$P^{h+1} = P^h P = \left( (1-\alpha^h)Q + \alpha^h I \right) \left( (1-\alpha)Q + \alpha I \right)$$
$$P^{h+1} = (1-\alpha^h)(1-\alpha)Q^2 + (1-\alpha^h)\alpha Q + \alpha^h(1-\alpha)Q + \alpha^{h+1} I$$

Substitute $Q^2 = Q$ and combine the $Q$ terms:

$$Q \left[ (1-\alpha^h - \alpha + \alpha^{h+1}) + (\alpha - \alpha^{h+1}) + (\alpha^h - \alpha^{h+1}) \right] = Q \left[ 1 - \alpha^{h+1} \right]$$

Thus, $P^{h+1} = (1-\alpha^{h+1})Q + \alpha^{h+1} I$. Converting this back to probabilities:

$$P(Y_{t+h} = C_i | Y_t = C_j) = (1-\alpha^h)p_i + \alpha^h \delta_{ij}$$

Part (c): Correlation Coefficient

Let $C_i = i$. Let $\mu = E[Y_t] = \sum_{i=1}^k i p_i$ and $\sigma^2 = Var(Y_t) = \sum_{i=1}^k i^2 p_i - \mu^2$. Because the marginal distribution is stationary, $E[Y_t] = E[Y_{t+h}] = \mu$ and $Var(Y_t) = Var(Y_{t+h}) = \sigma^2$.

We need $E[Y_t Y_{t+h}]$. By the Law of Iterated Expectations:

$$E[Y_t Y_{t+h}] = E_{Y_t}\left[ Y_t E[Y_{t+h} | Y_t] \right]$$

Calculate the inner conditional expectation using the $h$-step transition probabilities from part (b):

$$E[Y_{t+h} | Y_t = j] = \sum_{i=1}^k i \left[ (1-\alpha^h)p_i + \alpha^h \delta_{ij} \right] = (1-\alpha^h)\sum_{i=1}^k i p_i + \alpha^h j = (1-\alpha^h)\mu + \alpha^h j$$

Therefore, $E[Y_{t+h} | Y_t] = (1-\alpha^h)\mu + \alpha^h Y_t$. Substitute this back into the outer expectation:

$$E[Y_t Y_{t+h}] = E\left[ Y_t \left( (1-\alpha^h)\mu + \alpha^h Y_t \right) \right] = (1-\alpha^h)\mu E[Y_t] + \alpha^h E[Y_t^2]$$
$$E[Y_t Y_{t+h}] = (1-\alpha^h)\mu^2 + \alpha^h (\sigma^2 + \mu^2) = \mu^2 - \alpha^h\mu^2 + \alpha^h\sigma^2 + \alpha^h\mu^2 = \mu^2 + \alpha^h\sigma^2$$

Now, compute the covariance:

$$Cov(Y_t, Y_{t+h}) = E[Y_t Y_{t+h}] - E[Y_t]E[Y_{t+h}] = (\mu^2 + \alpha^h\sigma^2) - \mu^2 = \alpha^h\sigma^2$$

Finally, the correlation coefficient $\rho$ is:

$$Corr(Y_t, Y_{t+h}) = \frac{Cov(Y_t, Y_{t+h})}{\sqrt{Var(Y_t)Var(Y_{t+h})}} = \frac{\alpha^h\sigma^2}{\sigma \cdot \sigma} = \alpha^h$$
Final Answer / Q.E.D:
(a) The marginal distribution is strictly stationary: $P(Y_t = C_i) = p_i$.
(b) The conditional distribution is $P(Y_{t+h} = C_i | Y_t = C_j) = (1-\alpha^h)p_i + \alpha^h I(i=j)$.
(c) The correlation coefficient is $\rho = \alpha^h$.

📌 Q6 Fixed Effects Additive Block Design (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: Consider a block design in $b=v$ blocks of size $k=v+1$ with $v$ treatments such that the $i$-th treatment occurs twice in the $i$-th block and all other $v-1$ treatments occur once. Assume a fixed effects additive model.
(a) Find maximum number of estimable independent pairwise differences. Is it connected?
(b) Is the design orthogonal? Give reasons.
(c) Obtain the C-matrix. Find the BLUE of all estimable pairwise differences along with their variance.

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

This problem analyzes the properties of an incomplete/irregular block design using the Incidence Matrix $N$ and the Information Matrix $C$. We first construct $N$, where $N_{ij}$ denotes the occurrences of treatment $i$ in block $j$. Connectedness is determined by the rank of $C$ or by examining the incidence graph. Orthogonality requires a strict proportional relationship $N_{ij} = r_i k_j / n$. Finally, to find the Best Linear Unbiased Estimators (BLUE), we construct the matrix $C = R - NK^{-1}N^T$, analyze its eigenvalues, and solve the normal equations $C\hat{\tau} = Q$.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Step 1: Construct the Incidence Matrix $N$

There are $v$ treatments and $v$ blocks. The elements of $N$ are $N_{ii} = 2$ and $N_{ij} = 1$ for $i \neq j$. Thus, $N$ can be decomposed as:

$$N = J_v + I_v$$

where $J_v$ is a $v \times v$ matrix of all ones, and $I_v$ is the $v \times v$ identity matrix.
Replication sizes: $r_i = \sum_{j} N_{ij} = 2 + (v-1)1 = v+1$. So $R = (v+1)I_v$.
Block sizes: $k_j = \sum_{i} N_{ij} = v+1$. So $K = (v+1)I_v$.
Total observations: $n = \sum r_i = v(v+1)$.

Part (a): Connectedness and Estimability

A design is connected if there is a path between any two treatments in its incidence graph. Since $N_{ij} \ge 1$ for all $i, j$, every treatment appears in every block. This trivially forms a fully connected graph. Because the design is connected, the maximum number of linearly independent estimable pairwise differences of treatment effects is equal to its degrees of freedom:

$$\text{Max Estimable Differences} = v - 1$$

Part (b): Orthogonality

A block design is orthogonal if and only if the incidence matrix satisfies $N_{ij} = \frac{r_i k_j}{n}$ for all $i, j$. Let's test this condition:

$$\frac{r_i k_j}{n} = \frac{(v+1)(v+1)}{v(v+1)} = \frac{v+1}{v} = 1 + \frac{1}{v}$$

However, from our design definition, $N_{ij}$ only takes integer values of 1 or 2. Since $v$ is a positive integer (typically $v \ge 2$), $1 + 1/v$ is fractional and strictly between 1 and 2. Thus $N_{ij} \neq r_i k_j / n$. Therefore, the design is not orthogonal.

Part (c): Information Matrix C and BLUE

The C-matrix is defined as $C = R - NK^{-1}N^T$. Substituting our defined matrices:

$$C = (v+1)I - (J + I) \left[\frac{1}{v+1} I\right] (J + I)^T = (v+1)I - \frac{1}{v+1}(J + I)^2$$

Since $J^2 = vJ$, expanding the square gives $(J+I)^2 = J^2 + 2J + I = (v+2)J + I$.

$$C = (v+1)I - \frac{1}{v+1} \left[ (v+2)J + I \right] = \left( (v+1) - \frac{1}{v+1} \right)I - \frac{v+2}{v+1}J$$
$$C = \frac{(v+1)^2 - 1}{v+1}I - \frac{v+2}{v+1}J = \frac{v^2 + 2v}{v+1}I - \frac{v+2}{v+1}J = \frac{v+2}{v+1} \left( vI - J \right)$$

This $C$ matrix is totally symmetric (all diagonal elements are equal, all off-diagonals are equal). To find the BLUE $\hat{\tau}$, we solve the reduced normal equations $C\hat{\tau} = \mathbf{Q}$, where $\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{T} - NK^{-1}\mathbf{B}$ (adjusted treatment totals). To solve, we impose the standard restriction $\sum \hat{\tau}_i = 0$, which implies $J\hat{\tau} = \mathbf{0}$.

$$\frac{v+2}{v+1} (vI - J)\hat{\tau} = \mathbf{Q} \implies \frac{v(v+2)}{v+1}\hat{\tau} - \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{Q} \implies \hat{\tau} = \frac{v+1}{v(v+2)}\mathbf{Q}$$

The BLUE of a pairwise difference $\tau_i - \tau_j$ is simply:

$$\hat{\tau}_i - \hat{\tau}_j = \frac{v+1}{v(v+2)} (Q_i - Q_j)$$

The variance of this estimator for any normalized contrast $\mathbf{c}^T \hat{\tau}$ is $\sigma^2 \mathbf{c}^T C^{-} \mathbf{c}$, where $C^{-}$ is the generalized inverse. Because the non-zero eigenvalues of $C$ are all equal to $\theta = \frac{v(v+2)}{v+1}$, the variance of the difference between any two treatments is $\frac{2\sigma^2}{\theta}$:

$$Var(\hat{\tau}_i - \hat{\tau}_j) = \frac{2(v+1)}{v(v+2)}\sigma^2$$
Final Answer / Q.E.D:
(a) Max estimable differences = $v-1$. The design is fully connected.
(b) Not orthogonal, because $N_{ij} \in \{1, 2\}$, but $\frac{r_i k_j}{n} = 1 + \frac{1}{v}$, which does not equal $N_{ij}$.
(c) $C = \frac{v+2}{v+1}(vI - J)$. The BLUE is $\hat{\tau}_i - \hat{\tau}_j = \frac{v+1}{v(v+2)}(Q_i - Q_j)$, with Variance = $\frac{2(v+1)}{v(v+2)}\sigma^2$.

📌 Q7 Independence on a Uniform Unit Circle (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: Suppose that $(X,Y)$ follows a uniform distribution on the unit circle with the centre at the origin. Then prove or disprove the following statements.
(a) $|X|$ and $|Y|$ are independent.
(b) $\text{Sign}(X)$ and $\text{Sign}(Y)$ are independent, where $\text{Sign}(t) = 1, 0, -1$, respectively, for $t > 0, t = 0$, and $t < 0$.
(c) $|X|$ and $\text{Sign}(X)$ are independent.

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

The random vector $(X,Y)$ is uniformly distributed over the disk $D = \{(x,y) : x^2 + y^2 \leq 1\}$. The joint PDF is $f(x,y) = \frac{1}{\pi}$ on $D$, and $0$ otherwise. Because the boundary of the support region for $y$ depends on $x$ (specifically, $-\sqrt{1-x^2} \leq y \leq \sqrt{1-x^2}$), $X$ and $Y$ are naturally dependent. We will use the definition of independence—$P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$—and geometric probability (area ratios) to rigorously prove or disprove each statement.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Part (a): Independence of $|X|$ and $|Y|$

To disprove independence, we only need a single counter-example where the joint probability does not equal the product of the marginal probabilities. Let's look at the extreme edges of the support.

$$P\left(|X| > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) > 0 \quad \text{and} \quad P\left(|Y| > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) > 0$$

Both of these probabilities are strictly positive (they correspond to the areas of circular segments cut off by vertical and horizontal lines, respectively). However, consider their intersection:

$$P\left(|X| > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \text{ and } |Y| > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) = P\left(X^2 > \frac{1}{2} \text{ and } Y^2 > \frac{1}{2}\right)$$

If both conditions hold, then $X^2 + Y^2 > \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 1$. But the support of our distribution strictly requires $X^2 + Y^2 \leq 1$. Therefore, the probability of this intersection is exactly $0$.

$$0 = P\left(|X| > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cap |Y| > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \neq P\left(|X| > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) P\left(|Y| > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) > 0$$

Conclusion: Disproved. $|X|$ and $|Y|$ are dependent.

Part (b): Independence of $\text{Sign}(X)$ and $\text{Sign}(Y)$

Since the boundary of the circle has area $0$, $P(X=0) = P(Y=0) = 0$. The Sign functions effectively take values in $\{-1, 1\}$. By the radial symmetry of the circle across the axes, the probability of falling into any specific quadrant is equal to the area of that quadrant divided by the total area.

$$P(\text{Sign}(X) = 1, \text{Sign}(Y) = 1) = P(X > 0, Y > 0) = \frac{\text{Area of Quarter Circle}}{\text{Area of Full Circle}} = \frac{\pi/4}{\pi} = \frac{1}{4}$$

Now we find the marginal probabilities:

$$P(\text{Sign}(X) = 1) = P(X > 0) = \frac{\text{Area of Right Half}}{\pi} = \frac{\pi/2}{\pi} = \frac{1}{2}$$
$$P(\text{Sign}(Y) = 1) = P(Y > 0) = \frac{1}{2}$$

Checking the independence condition:

$$P(\text{Sign}(X) = 1, \text{Sign}(Y) = 1) = \frac{1}{4} = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = P(\text{Sign}(X) = 1) P(\text{Sign}(Y) = 1)$$

By symmetry, this holds for all four combinations of signs $(1,1), (1,-1), (-1,1), (-1,-1)$.

Conclusion: Proved. $\text{Sign}(X)$ and $\text{Sign}(Y)$ are independent.

Part (c): Independence of $|X|$ and $\text{Sign}(X)$

We need to check if the conditional distribution of $|X|$ given $\text{Sign}(X)$ is the same as the marginal distribution of $|X|$. Let $0 \leq x \leq 1$.

$$P(|X| \leq x \mid \text{Sign}(X) = 1) = \frac{P(|X| \leq x \text{ and } X > 0)}{P(X > 0)} = \frac{P(0 < X \leq x)}{1/2}$$

By symmetry of the unit circle across the y-axis, the probability mass on the right side ($X > 0$) perfectly mirrors the left side ($X < 0$).

$$P(0 < X \leq x) = P(-x \leq X < 0) = \frac{1}{2} P(-x \leq X \leq x) = \frac{1}{2} P(|X| \leq x)$$

Substitute this back into the conditional probability:

$$P(|X| \leq x \mid \text{Sign}(X) = 1) = \frac{\frac{1}{2} P(|X| \leq x)}{1/2} = P(|X| \leq x)$$

Similarly, $P(|X| \leq x \mid \text{Sign}(X) = -1) = P(|X| \leq x)$. Because the conditional CDF equals the marginal CDF regardless of the sign, the variables are independent.

Final Answer / Q.E.D:
(a) Disproved. They are dependent due to the coupled support boundary $X^2 + Y^2 \leq 1$.
(b) Proved. Quadrant probabilities naturally factorize into marginal half-plane probabilities.
(c) Proved. The magnitude's distribution is perfectly symmetric and identical on both the positive and negative halves of the $x$-axis.

📌 Q8 Maximum Likelihood and Consistency on a Diamond Support (15 Marks)

Problem Statement: Suppose that $\begin{bmatrix} X_1 \\ Y_1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} X_2 \\ Y_2 \end{bmatrix}, \dots, \begin{bmatrix} X_n \\ Y_n \end{bmatrix}$ are independent and identically distributed as uniform random vectors on the square with vertices $(\theta, 0), (0, \theta), (-\theta, 0), (0, -\theta)$.
(a) Find the Maximum Likelihood Estimator of $\theta$.
(b) Show that the estimator in (a) converges to $\theta$ in probability as $n \to \infty$.

🧠 Approach & Key Concepts

This is a non-regular estimation problem because the parameter $\theta$ determines the boundary of the support. The square described is a "diamond" shape centered at the origin, defined mathematically by the inequality $|x| + |y| \leq \theta$. We will construct the likelihood function, which will be a strictly decreasing function of $\theta$ subject to an indicator function bounding it from below. For part (b), we will prove consistency by deriving the Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) of the sufficient statistic and evaluating the limit of the probability $P(|\hat{\theta} - \theta| > \epsilon)$.

✍️ Step-by-Step Proof / Derivation

Part (a): Finding the MLE

First, calculate the area of the square support region. The square consists of 4 right-angled triangles with base $\theta$ and height $\theta$.

$$\text{Area} = 4 \times \left( \frac{1}{2} \cdot \theta \cdot \theta \right) = 2\theta^2$$

Because the distribution is uniform, the joint PDF for a single observation is:

$$f(x_i, y_i; \theta) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2\theta^2} & \text{if } |x_i| + |y_i| \leq \theta \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

The likelihood function for the sample of $n$ i.i.d. observations is the product of the individual PDFs:

$$L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{2\theta^2} \mathbf{1}_{\{|x_i| + |y_i| \leq \theta\}} = \frac{1}{2^n \theta^{2n}} \mathbf{1}_{\{ \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} (|x_i| + |y_i|) \leq \theta \}}$$

For $\theta \geq \max_{i} (|x_i| + |y_i|)$, the function $L(\theta) = \frac{1}{2^n \theta^{2n}}$ is a strictly decreasing function of $\theta$. To maximize $L(\theta)$, we must choose the smallest possible value for $\theta$ that satisfies the indicator condition.

$$\hat{\theta} = \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} (|X_i| + |Y_i|)$$

Part (b): Convergence in Probability (Consistency)

Let $Z_i = |X_i| + |Y_i|$. Thus, $\hat{\theta} = \max(Z_1, Z_2, \dots, Z_n) = Z_{(n)}$. We must show that for any $\epsilon > 0$, $\lim_{n \to \infty} P(|\hat{\theta} - \theta| \geq \epsilon) = 0$.

First, we find the CDF of $Z_i$. For $0 \leq z \leq \theta$, the event $Z_i \leq z$ corresponds to the point falling inside a smaller diamond with vertices at $(\pm z, 0)$ and $(0, \pm z)$. The area of this smaller diamond is $2z^2$. By geometric probability:

$$F_Z(z) = P(Z_i \leq z) = \frac{\text{Area}(|X| + |Y| \leq z)}{\text{Total Area}} = \frac{2z^2}{2\theta^2} = \left(\frac{z}{\theta}\right)^2$$

Now, we evaluate the probability of the estimator deviating from the true parameter. Since $Z_i \leq \theta$ by definition, the maximum order statistic $\hat{\theta}$ can never exceed $\theta$. Therefore, $|\hat{\theta} - \theta| = \theta - \hat{\theta}$.

$$P(|\hat{\theta} - \theta| \geq \epsilon) = P(\theta - \hat{\theta} \geq \epsilon) = P(\hat{\theta} \leq \theta - \epsilon)$$

The maximum of the sample is less than or equal to $\theta - \epsilon$ if and only if every individual $Z_i$ is less than or equal to $\theta - \epsilon$. Because the observations are independent:

$$P(\hat{\theta} \leq \theta - \epsilon) = P(Z_1 \leq \theta - \epsilon, \dots, Z_n \leq \theta - \epsilon) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(Z_i \leq \theta - \epsilon)$$
$$= \left[ F_Z(\theta - \epsilon) \right]^n = \left[ \left( \frac{\theta - \epsilon}{\theta} \right)^2 \right]^n = \left( 1 - \frac{\epsilon}{\theta} \right)^{2n}$$

Since $\epsilon > 0$, we have $0 \leq 1 - \frac{\epsilon}{\theta} < 1$. Taking the limit as $n \to \infty$ of a constant less than 1 raised to a growing power yields zero:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} P(|\hat{\theta} - \theta| \geq \epsilon) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left( 1 - \frac{\epsilon}{\theta} \right)^{2n} = 0$$
Final Answer / Q.E.D:
(a) The MLE is the maximum $L_1$ norm of the sample: $\hat{\theta} = \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} (|X_i| + |Y_i|)$.
(b) By deriving the CDF of the order statistic and taking the limit, we rigorously established that $P(|\hat{\theta} - \theta| \geq \epsilon) \to 0$, proving that $\hat{\theta} \xrightarrow{P} \theta$ (the estimator is consistent).
